# Team Health — Activity Ideas

Activities organized by driver. These are team-scope activities — use them in team meetings, focus days, or sprints. For individual-scope activities, see `engineer-motivation/references/activities.md`.

---

## Growth

Activities that develop the team's technical skills, process awareness, and learning culture.

**Tech talks from team members**
A 15-minute talk on any professional topic they choose. Start short — it's easy to prepare, not intimidating, and builds the habit. Combine with a personal talk (see Connection) for a 30-minute meeting.

**Discuss a technical article**
Choose a relevant article — better yet, let one of the developers choose. Read it together and discuss. Low prep, high signal on what the team cares about.

**Bring an insider domain expert**
Invite a senior IC or experienced EM from another team: DevOps, AI, frontend, security. Suggested questions: What are you working on? What's the most interesting thing you learned recently? What would you do differently if you were on our team?

**Technical debt review**
Ask the team what bothers them most. What 3 things would they fix first? Work together on an internal "technical roadmap." Works best when team members take ownership of specific improvements and drive them forward.

**Discuss the current development process**
A meeting dedicated to "how we work." Are you satisfied with the designs you receive? How are code reviews going? Is the deployment process working? With the PM present, this becomes a powerful cross-functional alignment moment.

**Solve bugs from another team's domain**
Take 1–2 bugs from an adjacent team (with their approval) and use it as an opportunity to understand a new domain. Best for teams that have been siloed for a while.

**Open-source work as a team**
Contribute to a library the team uses and loves, or create something of your own. Some engineers will love this. Even a few hours of progress is meaningful — a full sprint is ideal.

**Share your own tech-oriented lessons**
Share something from your own previous roles or companies. Works especially well coming from the manager — it helps the team know you better and establishes you as someone with hands-on technical history.

**Retro of retros**
Go over all retrospectives from the last 5–6 months. Summarize the main themes. What was addressed? What was forgotten? Use it to identify 1–2 things that are genuinely important and haven't been resolved yet.

## Connection

Activities that build trust, familiarity, and team cohesion.

**The 5 Things Game**
A getting-to-know-each-other game for competitive people. Works best for teams under 10. Choose 5–6 categories in advance (e.g., 3 favorite foods, 5 countries you've visited, 2 things nobody knows about you). Each person presents — others guess and keep score.

**Personal 15-minute talks**
Like tech talks, but about non-work topics — a hobby, a trip, something they care about. Combine with a tech talk for a 30-minute meeting. Humanizes teammates in a way that everyday work doesn't.

**Themed meetings**
Holiday- or tradition-themed meetings (Christmas, Eid, Diwali, Lunar New Year). Especially valuable on cross-cultural teams. An opportunity to share how you grew up and what you celebrate — builds familiarity across backgrounds.

**Play bowling**
Cheap, accessible, and surprisingly good for bonding across different personality types.

**Play a boardgame**
Works during a focus day outside the office or as a short activity during a team meeting. Choose something quick and easy to learn.

**Cooking workshop**
A team activity that tends to produce genuine fun, not performative fun. Works for both in-person and as a surprise for remote-first teams during off-sites.

## Impact

Activities that connect the team to the business, customers, and the people who depend on their work.

**Business review**
Share what you know that the team may not: last quarter's sales, struggles in other departments, financial context. Especially valuable during difficult periods — transparency in rough times builds more trust than transparency in good times.

**Roadmap discussion**
Ideally with the PM present. Not a replacement for a deep roadmap session — this is a periodic check-in (quarterly if your roadmap is 6–12 months). What's coming, what changed, and why.

**Invite your PM to a team meeting**
Make it special. Ask them to share their professional history, where they see the team in a year, and what they care about most in the roadmap. Opens dialogue that doesn't happen in planning meetings.

**Invite a salesperson to a team meeting**
Before starting a major project, have a sales rep share context: who is the customer, how was this project won, what matters to them. Engineers rarely get this context and almost always find it valuable.

**Invite a peer EM**
Ask them to share what their team is currently focused on, what they've learned recently, and how they see potential collaboration. Builds cross-team awareness and breaks silos.

**Bring your manager**
An intimate team-level conversation with your manager is different from an all-hands. Creates space for casual questions, direct dialogue, and signal that senior leadership is paying attention.

**Dogfood your own app together**
Find a creative way to use your own product as a team. If the CPO of Uber finds time to deliver groceries, you can find time to use your own product. Surfaces empathy gaps that no bug report captures.

**Quick-wins CS sprint**
A 2-week sprint based entirely on what the Customer Success team finds most painful. Work that normally loses to roadmap priorities — finally done, with a real audience who cares about every item.
