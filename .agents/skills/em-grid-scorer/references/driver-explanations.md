# The EM Grid — Driver Deep Dives

This file explains each of the three management drivers in depth. Claude references it when an EM asks for more detail on a specific column.

---

## Growth — Building for the Future

Growth activities invest in capability that doesn't pay off immediately. The payoff is resilience, quality, and speed — but weeks or months later.

**For yourself:** Learning new tools, receiving coaching, experimenting with new methodologies, staying technically relevant. EMs who stop growing personally become increasingly dependent on their team's judgment and increasingly opaque to the engineers they manage.

**For your people:** Career conversations, promotion advocacy, stretch assignments, developmental feedback. The goal is that each engineer is measurably more capable 6 months from now than they are today — because of something you did, not just because time passed.

**For the team:** Closing knowledge gaps, reducing bus factor, learning together from failures and experiments. A team that doesn't learn collectively accumulates invisible technical debt in the form of skill debt — until it surfaces as a crisis.

**For the org:** Contributing to engineering standards, mentoring outside your team, building the institutional knowledge that survives headcount changes.

**The growth trap:** Growth is the first thing that disappears under delivery pressure. It feels optional because the consequences of skipping it are delayed. Teams that stop growing show it in delivery quality, attrition, and brittleness — never in sprint velocity.

## Impact — Producing Results Now

Impact activities create direct value: something ships, a metric moves, a decision gets made, a stakeholder gets what they need.

**For yourself:** Writing documents that get used, building tools that others depend on, making key decisions, producing work that influences the direction of the org.

**For your people:** Connecting engineers to business outcomes. The goal isn't just shipping — it's shipping things that matter and are recognized. Engineers who never see their work land lose motivation slowly and invisibly.

**For the team:** Consistent, reliable delivery. The "always green" principle: set minimal, confident goals and achieve them every sprint. A team that consistently hits its goals creates compounding trust with leadership. A team that consistently misses them creates compounding pressure, regardless of effort.

**For the org:** Making your team visible, helping other departments achieve their goals, speaking in business language (retention, gross margin, churn) rather than engineering language (velocity, tickets, PRs).

**The impact trap:** Most EMs over-index on Team×Impact and under-invest in every other impact cell. Shipping features isn't the same as creating business impact. An EM who only manages delivery is not managing — they're coordinating.

**Business impact levels (from most basic to most advanced):**
1. You and your team have access to product metrics and know what they mean
2. You can connect specific team decisions to specific business metrics
3. You use business language fluently in stakeholder conversations and it changes outcomes

## Connection — Building Relationships

Connection activities build the human infrastructure that makes everything else work: trust, psychological safety, goodwill, and a sense of belonging.

**For yourself:** Your professional network outside the company. This is where you get market signal, honest peer advice, and hiring leads before they become postings. External connections compound — they're worth almost nothing in month 1 and enormously valuable in year 3.

**For your people:** Knowing engineers as human beings, not just as producers. This means remembering their lives, noticing when they're off, recognizing their contributions specifically and genuinely. Psychological safety — where people feel safe to raise problems, make mistakes, and be honest — is the #1 predictor of team effectiveness (Google's Project Aristotle). It's primarily created by the team leader.

**For the team:** The social fabric that makes people want to show up. Team meetings that go beyond tasks, focus days, celebrations, shared humor. Teams with strong connection survive personnel changes, hard sprints, and pivots much better than teams held together only by shared work.

**For the org:** Being known and liked across the company. The "new person rule" (one coffee chat per week with someone new) compounds into an internal network that makes everything easier — stakeholder negotiations, cross-team projects, hiring referrals, and political air cover.

**The connection trap:** Connection activities feel like the first thing to cut when time is short. They are. But disconnected engineers are the first to leave, the last to flag problems, and the least likely to go above and beyond when the team needs it. Connection isn't a nice-to-have — it's the substrate everything else runs on.

**Positioning levels for org connection (from basic to advanced):**
1. People know you exist — you're present in cross-team channels, you show up to company events
2. People appreciate you — you're curious about their work, you remember details, you follow up
3. People trust you — you help others achieve their goals proactively, you're the person they think of when they need a technical or leadership perspective
